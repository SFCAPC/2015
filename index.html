2015 Annual Report

The San Francisco Child Abuse Prevention Center stands at the intersection of child advocacy and child safety.

Protecting children is, and has always been, the singular goal we work towards. By ensuring that today’s children are afforded childhoods — safety, health, love, and laughter — we create better tomorrows. It is when we, as a collective community, fail to protect children from abuse that we fail not just the child, but the entire community and the promise of our society’s future.

We protect children by creating strong families, by building engaged communities, and by intentionally designing a society that respects and protects its most vulnerable members. That is what we are working to do, everyday.

This is why we have family therapy, weekly dinners, community education classes, and city-wide awareness campaigns. It is why we visit families in their homes and children in their schools. It’s why we train adults how to notice and report suspected abuse. It’s why we provide food bags and bus tokens. It’s why we teach parenting classes, and why we work with the city to keep families together. It’s why we have so many passionate volunteers, and it’s why the staff works with such dedication and devotion.

This is why the Prevention Center made the decision to purchase its building on Third Street in the Bayview — and made great strides in raising the necessary funds to do so. As San Francisco’s real estate prices soar — and community organizations are being forced out — it is essential that San Francisco preserves a permanent home for the Children's Advocacy Center, and a sanctuary for the children of this city.

We do this because we know when families are able to meet their basic needs, kids are safer. When parents understand the fundamental of child development, kids are safer. And when parents have a support network to help them weather the ups and downs of city life, kids are safer. We do this because we know that when children and community members are empowered to speak up and speak out, we save childhoods — in fact, we save lives.

We do this all in pursuit of a singular goal: to create a community where childhoods are sacred and child abuse is no longer a concept. It’s not just our mission, it’s our mandate.

Our Stories

Here's why prevention matters. Three stories about how education, support services, and collaboration can protect children and stop child abuse.

Empowering Children

Erika began explaining safe and unsafe touches to the class. As she explained the difference between fighting with words and fighting with bodies, Jaqueline was quick to blurt out her story.

Jaqueline was frozen with fear, watching the fight between her mom and stepdad. Her little sister’s tears pled for the yelling and hitting to stop, and to Jaqueline’s relief, the crying disrupted the fighting and there was an end (for the time-being).

Although the feud had stopped, Jaqueline’s mother still raged. Later that day, Jaqueline’s mother shoved her little sister out of the way so forcefully that she smashed her head on the ground.

After class, Erika talked privately with Jaqueline and began to untangle her story — as Erika delicately pressed for more information, more details of her home life and her mother’s actions surfaced. She had been hit with a belt, a shoe, an open-hand — and she had the markings to prove it.

Jaqueline’s grandmother was in on it too, egging on the abuse, saying, “hit her until she wets herself.”

After their conversation, Erika worked with Jaqueline’s teacher and the school’s social worker to ensure a report was made to Child Protective Services and to make certain that Jaqueline would be helped and protected.

Erika Pappas, a child safety instructor with the San Francisco Child Abuse Prevention Center, was in Jaqueline’s 5th grade classroom teaching the children about safety and about how to protect themselves from harm. It was January, and this was just one of the dozens of classrooms Erika visited during the year.

In 2015, the Community Education team implemented a system to assess and evaluate San Francisco’s elementary schools, rating and ranking the level of risk their students were at for child abuse. This data-driven approach to targeting at-risk schools for Child Safety Awareness classes helps to ensure that this educational programming achieves maximum impact.

“The students are starting to really use more words to tell classmates that they don’t like something or not to touch them.”

–Teacher, San Francisco Unified School District

Providing Support

He was becoming as sad as she was. Ryan’s mom was always sad. She never wanted to play with him or take him to the park, or to see a movie. Ryan’s mom was depressed and Ryan was following her model. He wasn’t interested in playing handball or kickball or any of the other games the other kids were playing during recess.

As Sharon watched her son sadden, she knew she needed help, but she was unsure what help would look like and had no idea where to find it. After a brief internet search, Sharon started coming to the San Francisco Child Abuse Prevention Center for guidance. First casually during open drop-in hours and for weekly community dinners. Within the walls of the firehouse, she found solidarity with other struggling parents.

As her comfort with the Prevention Center grew, she began conversations with the staff members about her family and her inability to be a present parent and help her son. After several months of working with staff and attending support groups, Sharon finally realized she needed therapy, and a divorce — and Mike, Ryan’s dad, agreed that their marriage was having a negative impact on the entire family.

Sharon choose a private therapist, and Mike came to the Prevention Center for counseling. After nearly a year of working with the Prevention Center, Sharon and Mike found their way back to being happy individuals and engaged parents with a new-found knowledge of how to care for their son. And, Ryan began making new friends and was back in the four square game.

In 2015, the Prevention Center continued to refine its intensive services model — which surrounds families with services to strengthen their protective factors — and began to share this successful model with organizations around the city and across the country. This approach is proving that families, given the right support, tools, and resource can not just rebound but thrive.

"This place has helped me and made me the person that I am. This is the place I go because I know that everyone will help me. I feel respected and safe."

–Mother & client

Building a System

As the team of colleagues from the San Francisco Child Abuse Prevention Center, the Human Services Agency, and others gathered and settled into their seats, casual conversation gave way to the city’s harsh realities. San Francisco is amongst the highest regions in the country for commercially sexually exploited children (CSEC) — sex trafficking victims. It was the mandate of this team to figure out how to ensure these victims receive the services and support they need.

It was a new team with a new directive, as just recently the State of California placed commercially sexually exploited children under the purview of the child welfare system, rather than the juvenile justice system.

On this morning, the team had united to discuss the case of a young boy, Paolo (we’ve changed his name to protect his identity), a victim of repeated exploitation who was in need of a variety of services. Responding to Paolo quickly and appropriately was essential to his healing. However, given the newness of the law, the team had not yet been able to put into place policies, protocols, and procedures for how to share case reports, files, and information while maintaining a victim's privacy rights.

But victims like Paolo cannot wait for policy updates, so at this moment, the team did their best to organize support and coordinate care — and it was not easy. Confidentiality laws prevented them from even speaking his name out loud.

Thankfully, the Children's Advocacy Center of San Francisco recently implemented an Information Sharing Agreement creating clear guidelines about how, with whom, and when client information can be shared — a foundation the new team could build off of.

In 2015, under the leadership of the Prevention Center, the partners of the Children's Advocacy Center enacted an Information Sharing Agreement which formally established clear policies around information sharing — enabling better coordination of services, better treatment management, and allowing for open discussions — while respecting the victim’s privacy. This crucial work blazed the trail for the CSEC team to begin creating an Information Sharing Agreement of their own.
